Learning Turkish

Elias Alves Moura <eliasmoura.alves@gmail.com> v1.0, 2018-04-12 :keywords: learning, turkish, türkçe, languages, noobkotto :description: A few thoughts on learning Turkish.

The Turkish language or Turkey were things that I never thought of having an interest of. But after I picked up a good level of knowledge in English and got myself fascinated with learning a foreign language, I can’t hold the thought of how lame it would be knowing just one or two languages. Now, I feel the need to know other foreign languages and cultures. That might have turned into some kind of addiction. One that I don’t mind having.

Being able to watch a video, read a newspaper or book, listen to audio in English is awesome. Talking with people in English is amazing. Although I barely do that, it still feels good. At the moment, I can do that in a very small scale in Turkish.

After I listened to a few Youtube videos in Turkish, I couldn’t hold myself but be amazed by the language. It sound incredible and fun. The grammar is challenging with the agglutinating nature of the language. Appending suffixes to a word is, however, an interesting characteristic of the language. We have some of it in Portuguese; for example, when you want to say "little man", you can just say "homemzinho". That "zinho(a)" suffix creates the meaning of "little" in Portuguese.

In Turkish, however, the use of suffix are much more complex and bring deep meanings than what you have in an other language I know of. For example, if you want to say "I’m at home" in Turkish, it would be "Evdeyim". Breaking the word we have:

  • ev → home/house

  • de → in/at/on

  • (y)im → I am

With this simple example is easy to see how powerful the agglutination of the Turkish language can be. It is interesting and at the same time very challenging to non-native speakers. You can easily get yourself frustrated trying to break down the words to find their roots and suffixes. Leaning the grammar is by no means easy.

Portuguese was a languages that I hated during all my time in school. When I started to use it by myself and learn more about other languages, I started to appreciate my mother tongue. I think I got an appreciation to all languages after I developed interest in languages. Pronunciation is something I feel I should be focusing more, but I never do it. Turkish has many friendly sounds for Brazilian Portuguese speakers. But, that doesn’t means I can easily pronounce all the words in Turkish. Learning how to say the ı sound properly was challenging; I think I am not that good at it still. I try to practice with a few set of words like kırmızı I also had help with the pronunciation of those words. A friend recorded a set of words for me and I tried to imitate it.

The word that I had most difficult was kırmzı. All those "ı" seemed impossible for me to pronounce. But with lot of practice and help of my Turkish friends I managed to pronounce it in an acceptable manner. Although not as fast as I would hope, I feel like I’m improving.

I have a lot to learn, but I`m getting somewhere and that makes me happy.
